\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate DIAL along three axes: does environment-aware gating
Pareto-dominate fixed-direction baselines
(\S\ref{sec:main-results}); which components drive the gains
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}); and do the Two-Source Model's predictions
hold on held-out data (\S\ref{sec:theory-verification}).

\paragraph{Environments.} We evaluate on 6 environments:
HotpotQA~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa},
APPS~\cite{hendrycks2021apps},
WebShop~\cite{yao2022webshop},
FEVER~\cite{thorne2018fever},
TWExpress~\cite{jansen2023twexpress}, and
Plancraft~\cite{dagan2024plancraft}. 
% , spanning QA, code generation,
% web navigation, fact verification, text games, and crafting planning.
These cover the full range of the Two-Source
Model, from pure Type~I (FEVER) through mixed (WebShop) to
Type~D (APPS), including extreme rollout properties
(TWExpress: rollout-safe; Plancraft: rollout-harmful).
Full environment specifications are in
Appendix~\ref{app:environment}.

\paragraph{Baselines.} All methods share the same agent and
optimizer $T$; only the gating decision differs.
We include two bounds: \emph{base\_only}, which never triggers
the optimizer, and \emph{always\_trigger}.
Six fixed-direction baselines all assume that high uncertainty
warrants additional compute:
CaTS~\cite{cats2025}, SEAG~\cite{seag2025},
CoRefine~\cite{corefine2026}, CATTS~\cite{catts2026},
AUQ~\cite{auq2026}, and s1\_budget~\cite{s1_2025}.
Full specifications are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-comparison}.

\paragraph{Metrics.} SR (success rate, $\uparrow$) and Cost
($\times$base, $\downarrow$): total deployment tokens normalized by
base\_only tokens per episode. This metric counts \emph{all} LLM
calls, including base proposer, gate overhead, and rollout calls, ensuring that per-step
overhead is not hidden. Calibration or exploration overhead is
excluded for all methods.
A method Pareto-dominates another if $\text{SR} \geq$ and
$\text{Cost} \leq$ with at least one strict inequality.
Token cost constants are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:cost}.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}



\textbf{DIAL Pareto-dominates fixed-direction baselines.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows the SR--Cost Pareto frontier across
all 6 environments on Qwen3-4B; full results across three
backbones are in Appendix~\ref{app:full-results}.
Three patterns emerge. First, \emph{fixed-direction baselines
catastrophically fail when the assumed direction is wrong}:
on FEVER, a Type~I task, CATTS reaches only 32.2\% SR, below
the 37.0\% no-trigger baseline, yet costs 16.46$\times$ base
from its $K{=}5$ per-step voting overhead.
Second, \emph{DIAL achieves competitive SR at substantially
lower cost} when rollouts help: on HotpotQA, DIAL reaches
95.2\% SR at 8.02$\times$ versus always\_trigger's 97.0\%
at 10.63$\times$; on WebShop, DIAL matches always\_trigger
in SR (43.8 vs.\ 43.0\%) at less than half the cost,
2.50$\times$ versus 5.56$\times$; on APPS, DIAL outperforms
the best fixed-direction baseline, s1\_budget at 69.0\%, by
4 points at comparable cost. Third, \emph{in rollout-harmful
environments DIAL reverts toward base\_only}: on Plancraft,
where always\_trigger itself loses 7.0 points, DIAL recovers
near base\_only at 23.3 vs.\ 29.8\%, while aggressive
baselines such as s1\_budget fall below base\_only at 16.3\%.
Across these patterns, DIAL interpolates between the two
bounds in a headroom-dependent way; the full bounds comparison
is in Appendix~\ref{app:bounds-comparison}.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

The main results show \emph{that} DIAL works; we now ask
\emph{why}. We isolate the role of direction along two axes:
its sign (direction reversal) and the gate's capacity to exploit
it (gate complexity). LLM feature and regularizer ablations
appear in Appendix~\ref{app:llm-features} and
Appendix~\ref{app:regularizer-ablation}.

\textbf{Direction is a first-order determinant of gate quality.}
To isolate the effect of direction, we reverse the sign of all
learned weights in DIAL's fitted gate while keeping everything
else unchanged, constructing an adversarially wrong-direction
gate that preserves ranking structure but inverts trigger
semantics. Table~\ref{tab:wrong-direction} shows that on
strong-signal environments with $|\rho_{\text{ent}}| > 0.4$,
reversal collapses SR by 23--37\%; on weak-signal Plancraft
($|\rho_{\text{ent}}|{=}0.016$) the effect is negligible. The damage scales
with signal strength: wrong direction is not a calibration
error but a structural failure. This instantiates the
wrong-direction harm result of Appendix~\ref{app:proof-necessity}:
a sharper gate fires precisely on the states where
computation has negative value.

\textbf{Gate capacity does not substitute for direction.}
A natural objection is that DIAL's gains come from gate
simplicity rather than learned direction. With correct
direction, a logistic gate (5 features), a 2-layer MLP
(5 features), and a hidden-state probe (2560-d) all reach
$\approx$95\% SR on HotpotQA: capacity adds $<$1\%. With wrong
direction, the ordering inverts: the MLP drops to 45.3\%,
\emph{below} the wrong-direction logistic at 62.0\%. Direction
consistently outweighs capacity, which is why DIAL's minimal
linear gate suffices.



\subsection{Two-Source Model Verification}
\label{sec:theory-verification}

Following~\citet{schaeffer2023emergent}, we derive three testable
predictions of the Two-Source Model (\S\ref{sec:toy-model}) and
verify each on held-out data: within-episode temporal dynamics
(P1), within-task causal manipulation (P2), and cross-environment
signal identity (P3).


\paragraph{P1: Temporal dynamics.}

The Two-Source Model predicts that $\rho$ should decrease over
the episode: as the agent gathers information, Type~D states
are resolved and late-step uncertainty increasingly reflects
residual Type~I states.
Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-shift} confirms this. In HotpotQA,
$\rho$ shifts from $-0.176$ (early) to $-0.437$ (late). In
WebShop, the strong positive early-phase signal
($\rho{=}{+}0.285$) vanishes in the late phase. In APPS,
$\rho$ shifts from non-significant positive ($+$0.102, $p{=}0.084$)
to weak negative ($-$0.144). Full numerical results are in
Appendix~\ref{app:temporal}.

\paragraph{P2: Controlled information manipulation.}
P1 verifies a within-episode dynamic; we now intervene on
$p_I$ directly for \emph{causal} evidence. We construct two
interventions on HotpotQA: \textbf{InfoPoor} restricts
retrievable evidence to a small passage subset (forcing
Type~I), and \textbf{InfoRich} prepends gold-standard evidence
to the prompt (forcing Type~D). Table~\ref{tab:controlled}
reports the result: restricting evidence makes
\texttt{step\_count} dominant ($\rho{=}{-}0.608$) while entropy
is weak; injecting gold evidence makes entropy dominant
($\rho{=}{+}0.311$) while \texttt{step\_count} weakens. As the
only test in this section that intervenes on the latent
mixture rather than observing it, P2 confirms that signal
identity is a function of information structure.
%, not an
%environment-specific constant.

\paragraph{P3: Signal identity alignment.}
The Two-Source Model further predicts that, observed across
environments, the strongest signal should reflect the dominant
state type: information-sufficiency proxies in Type~I
environments, decision-complexity proxies in Type~D
environments. Table~\ref{tab:signal-identity} confirms this:
\texttt{step\_count} dominates in Type~I-leaning environments,
while \texttt{num\_available\_actions} and other choice-related
features dominate in WebShop. Per-environment LASSO selection
results are in Appendix~\ref{app:feature-selection}.

The three tests provide convergent evidence across distinct
modes of inference. Cross-setting $\rho$ consistency at fixed
backbone is reported
in Appendix~\ref{app:cross-setting}; multi-backbone
verification in Appendix~\ref{app:multi-backbone} and stratified
analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:stratified} provide additional
robustness checks.
