\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate EAAG along three axes: does environment-aware gating
Pareto-dominate fixed-direction baselines
(\S\ref{sec:main-results}); which components drive the gains
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}); and do the Two-Source Model's predictions
hold on held-out data (\S\ref{sec:theory-verification}).

\paragraph{Environments.} We evaluate on 6 environments:
HotpotQA~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa},
APPS~\cite{hendrycks2021apps},
WebShop~\cite{yao2022webshop},
FEVER~\cite{thorne2018fever},
TWExpress~\cite{jansen2023twexpress}, and
Plancraft~\cite{dagan2024plancraft}, spanning QA, code generation,
web navigation, fact verification, text games, and crafting planning.
These cover the full range of the Two-Source
Model, from pure Type~I (FEVER) through mixed (WebShop) to
Type~D (APPS), including extreme rollout properties
(TWExpress: rollout-safe; Plancraft: rollout-harmful).
Full environment specifications are in
Appendix~\ref{app:environment}.

\paragraph{Baselines.} All methods share the same agent and
optimizer $T$; only the gating decision differs.
We include three bounds: \emph{base\_only}, which never triggers
the optimizer; \emph{always\_trigger}; and \emph{oracle}.
Six fixed-direction baselines all assume that high uncertainty
warrants additional compute:
CaTS~\cite{cats2025}, SEAG~\cite{seag2025},
CoRefine~\cite{corefine2026}, CATTS~\cite{catts2026},
AUQ~\cite{auq2026}, and s1\_budget~\cite{s1_2025}.
%We also evaluate BSW, which intentionally reverses the learned
%direction to quantify the cost of direction mismatch.
Full specifications are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-comparison}.

\paragraph{Metrics.} SR (success rate, $\uparrow$) and Cost
($\times$base, $\downarrow$): total deployment tokens normalized by
base\_only tokens per episode. This metric counts \emph{all} LLM
calls---base proposer, gate overhead (CATTS $K{=}5$ voting, AUQ
confidence queries), and rollout calls---ensuring that per-step
overhead is not hidden. Calibration or exploration overhead is
excluded for all methods.
A method Pareto-dominates another if $\text{SR} \geq$ and
$\text{Cost} \leq$ with at least one strict inequality.
Token cost constants are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:cost}.




\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_pareto.pdf}
\caption{SR vs.\ Cost ($\times$base) across all 6 environments
(Qwen3-4B). Each method uses a distinct marker shape.
Shaded region: dominated by EAAG (lower SR \emph{and} higher cost).
Full numerical results across three backbones are in
Table~\ref{tab:full-results}.}
\label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{EAAG Pareto-dominates fixed-direction baselines.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows the SR--Cost Pareto frontier across
all 6 environments on Qwen3-4B; full numerical results across
three backbones are in
Table~\ref{tab:full-results} (Appendix~\ref{app:full-results}).

Three patterns emerge. First, fixed-direction baselines
catastrophically fail when the assumed direction is wrong: on
FEVER (Type~I), CATTS achieves only 32.2\% SR (below the 37.0\%
no-trigger baseline) yet costs 16.46$\times$base due to its
$K{=}5$ per-step voting overhead.
Second, EAAG achieves competitive SR at substantially lower cost.
On WebShop, EAAG reaches 43.8\% SR at 2.50$\times$base, matching
always\_trigger (43.0\%) at less than half the cost
(5.56$\times$). On APPS, EAAG outperforms the best baseline
(s1\_budget, 69.0\%) by 4\,pp at comparable cost.
Third, in the rollout-harmful environment Plancraft
($\Delta{=}{-}7.0$), EAAG learns to suppress triggering and
recovers near base\_only performance (23.3\% vs.\ 29.8\%),
while most baselines that trigger aggressively fall below
base\_only (s1\_budget: 16.3\%).

\textbf{EAAG bridges the gap between base\_only and
always\_trigger.}
Table~\ref{tab:bounds-comparison} compares EAAG against the two
bounds across all 6 environments on Qwen3-4B. EAAG recovers
70--97\% of the oracle headroom ($\Delta$) in 4 of 6 environments
at 1.81--8.02$\times$base cost, substantially below
always\_trigger (2.15--10.63$\times$). In WebShop, EAAG
\emph{exceeds} always\_trigger SR (43.8\% vs.\ 43.0\%) at 45\% of
the cost. In the rollout-harmful environment Plancraft
($\Delta{=}{-}7.0$), EAAG correctly learns to suppress triggering
(3.63$\times$ vs.\ always\_trigger 6.13$\times$), losing only
6.5\,pp relative to base\_only while always\_trigger loses
7.0\,pp.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

The main results show \emph{that} EAAG works; we now ask
\emph{why}. We ablate the two key components: direction learning
and the LLM feature layer.

\textbf{Direction is the primary determinant of gate quality.}
Intentionally reversing the learned direction (BSW ablation)
causes large SR drops in every environment with strong signal:
FEVER falls from 49.8\% to 13.0\% ($-$36.8\,pp),
HotpotQA from 95.2\% to 58.2\% ($-$37.0\,pp), and WebShop
from 43.8\% to 20.6\% ($-$23.2\,pp). In contrast,
weak-signal environments like Plancraft ($|\rho|{=}0.016$)
are unaffected ($+$2.2\,pp). The degradation scales with
signal strength: wrong direction is not a minor calibration
error but a structural failure, as
Proposition~\ref{prop:necessity} predicts.
Full per-environment results are in
Appendix~\ref{app:wrong-direction}.

\textbf{The LLM feature layer is an automation convenience, not
a core component.} Table~\ref{tab:llm-ablation} compares EAAG
with and without LLM-generated features. FEVER is the only
environment where LLM features contribute meaningfully
(+9.1\,pp), where task-specific signals (\texttt{has\_claim},
\texttt{text\_length}) extend the universal feature pool.
In the remaining environments, removing LLM features changes SR
by ${<}$2\,pp; in Plancraft and APPS the contribution is
effectively zero. The LLM layer is best viewed as an automation
convenience for zero-shot deployment rather than a component
the method depends on.

Beyond direction and features, EAAG's trigger rate also adapts
per environment, ranging from 73\% (TWExpress) to ${<}$20\%
(late-step Plancraft). This behavior emerges from direction
learning alone and is analyzed in
Appendix~\ref{app:trigger-rate}.


\subsection{Two-Source Model Verification}
\label{sec:theory-verification}

The ablations confirm EAAG's practical advantage; we now test
whether the Two-Source Model (\S\ref{sec:toy-model}) makes correct
predictions on held-out data. Following
\cite{schaeffer2023emergent}, we derive three testable predictions
and verify each:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=\textbf{P\arabic*:}]
\item \textbf{Temporal dynamics.} Within the same environment,
  $\rho$ should \emph{decrease} over the episode: early steps mix
  Type~I and Type~D uncertainty, while late steps, after
  information-gathering attempts, isolate the residual Type~I
  component.
\item \textbf{Cross-environment consistency.} Environments with
  similar task structure should exhibit similar $\rho$
  (e.g., FEVER $\approx$ HotpotQA, both search-based QA).
\item \textbf{Signal identity alignment.} In Type~I environments,
  the strongest signal should measure information sufficiency; in
  Type~D environments, decision complexity.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig_temporal_shift.pdf}
\caption{P1 verification: $\rho$(entropy, U) shifts from early to
late steps. Blue: early (step $\leq$ median), red: late.
Values on each bar show the Spearman $\rho$ for that phase.
Plancraft is omitted because its entropy signal is too weak
($|\rho|{=}0.016$) for a meaningful early/late comparison.}
\label{fig:temporal-shift}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{P1: Temporal dynamics.}
Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-shift} shows that within each
environment, $\rho$ shifts between early and late steps. In
HotpotQA, $\rho$ moves from $-0.176$ (early) to $-0.437$ (late):
late-step entropy reflects deeper information poverty after failed
evidence retrieval. In WebShop, the strong positive early-phase
signal ($\rho{=}{+}0.285$) vanishes in the late phase. In APPS,
the positive $\rho$ strengthens over steps as decision complexity
accumulates. The pattern holds across all environments with
sufficient signal (Appendix~\ref{app:temporal}).

\paragraph{P2: Cross-environment consistency.}
Same-family environments share the same direction
(Table~\ref{tab:signal-discovery}).
FEVER and HotpotQA, both search-based QA, share negative $\rho$;
WebShop and APPS, both involving active choice among alternatives,
share positive $\rho$. Magnitude differences within each pair
reflect the degree of Type~I contamination.

\paragraph{P3: Signal identity alignment.}
The strongest signal per environment matches the Two-Source Model
prediction (Appendix~\ref{app:signal-alignment}):
\texttt{step\_count} dominates in Type~I environments as an
information-accumulation proxy, while
\texttt{num\_available\_actions} dominates in WebShop as a
decision-complexity proxy. A controlled manipulation experiment
within HotpotQA further confirms that signal identity shifts
with information structure (Appendix~\ref{app:controlled}).

All three predictions are supported. Additional robustness
checks are in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}: multi-backbone
verification (\S\ref{app:multi-backbone}), stratified analysis
(\S\ref{app:stratified}), and gate complexity ablations
(\S\ref{app:gate-complexity}).
