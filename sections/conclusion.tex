\section{Conclusion}

All existing adaptive gating methods for LLM agents rest on an
implicit assumption: that uncertainty signals have a fixed
relationship with compute value. We show that this assumption is
incorrect. Across eight environments and three model backbones,
signal--utility direction reverses, and once direction is
misaligned, improved calibration \emph{worsens} performance by
more precisely selecting harmful states. A two-source uncertainty
model grounded in Simpson's paradox explains when and why reversal
occurs, and a necessity proof establishes that direction discovery
is required for cross-environment non-negative value of computation.
EAAG instantiates this principle through exploration, LLM-based
reasoning, and sparse direction learning, achieving adaptive gating
with zero additional per-step overhead. Across all evaluated
settings, EAAG dominates fixed-direction baselines, with the full
range of gating behavior---from aggressive triggering to near-total
suppression---emerging from direction learning alone.
