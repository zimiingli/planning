\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate DIAL along three axes: does environment-aware gating
Pareto-dominate fixed-direction baselines
(\S\ref{sec:main-results}); which components drive the gains
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}); and do the Two-Source Model's predictions
hold on held-out data (\S\ref{sec:theory-verification}).

\paragraph{Environments.} We evaluate on 6 environments:
HotpotQA~\cite{yang2018hotpotqa},
APPS~\cite{hendrycks2021apps},
WebShop~\cite{yao2022webshop},
FEVER~\cite{thorne2018fever},
TWExpress~\cite{jansen2023twexpress}, and
Plancraft~\cite{dagan2024plancraft}. 
% , spanning QA, code generation,
% web navigation, fact verification, text games, and crafting planning.
These cover the full range of the Two-Source
Model, from pure Type~I (FEVER) through mixed (WebShop) to
Type~D (APPS), including extreme rollout properties
(TWExpress: rollout-safe; Plancraft: rollout-harmful).
Full environment specifications are in
Appendix~\ref{app:environment}.

\paragraph{Baselines.} All methods share the same agent and
optimizer $T$; only the gating decision differs.
We include two bounds: \emph{base\_only}, which never triggers
the optimizer, and \emph{always\_trigger}.
Six fixed-direction baselines all assume that high uncertainty
warrants additional compute:
CaTS~\cite{cats2025}, SEAG~\cite{seag2025},
CoRefine~\cite{corefine2026}, CATTS~\cite{catts2026},
AUQ~\cite{auq2026}, and s1\_budget~\cite{s1_2025}.
Full specifications are in Appendix~\ref{app:method-comparison}.

\paragraph{Metrics.} SR (success rate, $\uparrow$) and Cost
($\times$base, $\downarrow$): total deployment tokens normalized by
base\_only tokens per episode. This metric counts \emph{all} LLM
calls, including base proposer, gate overhead (CATTS $K{=}5$ voting,
AUQ confidence queries), and rollout calls, ensuring that per-step
overhead is not hidden. Calibration or exploration overhead is
excluded for all methods.
A method Pareto-dominates another if $\text{SR} \geq$ and
$\text{Cost} \leq$ with at least one strict inequality.
Token cost constants are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:cost}.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}



\textbf{DIAL Pareto-dominates fixed-direction baselines.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows the SR--Cost Pareto frontier across
all 6 environments on Qwen3-4B. Full results across three
backbones are in Appendix~\ref{app:full-results}.

Three patterns emerge. First, \emph{fixed-direction baselines
catastrophically fail when the assumed direction is wrong}: on
FEVER (Type~I), CATTS achieves only 32.2\% SR (below the 37.0\%
no-trigger baseline) yet costs 16.46$\times$base due to its
$K{=}5$ per-step voting overhead.
Second, \emph{DIAL achieves competitive SR at substantially lower
cost}. On WebShop, DIAL reaches 43.8\% SR at 2.50$\times$base,
matching always\_trigger (43.0\%) at less than half the cost
(5.56$\times$). On APPS, DIAL outperforms the best baseline
(s1\_budget, 69.0\%) by 4\,pp at comparable cost.
Third, \emph{in rollout-harmful environments, DIAL learns to
suppress triggering}: in Plancraft ($\Delta{=}{-}7.0$), DIAL
recovers near base\_only performance (23.3\% vs.\ 29.8\%),
while most baselines that trigger aggressively fall below
base\_only (s1\_budget: 16.3\%).

% FEVER remains DIAL's weakest environment: while DIAL achieves the
% highest SR (49.8\%), its cost (16.51$\times$base) is also the
% highest, a limitation we analyze in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\textbf{DIAL adapts its behavior to each environment.}
Table~\ref{tab:bounds-comparison} compares DIAL against the two
bounds on Qwen3-4B. In environments with positive rollout
headroom (HotpotQA, WebShop, TWExpress, APPS), DIAL achieves
SR close to always\_trigger at substantially lower cost. In the
rollout-harmful environment Plancraft ($\Delta{=}{-}7.0$), DIAL
learns to suppress triggering, losing only 6.5\,pp relative to
base\_only while always\_trigger loses 7.0\,pp. FEVER remains
the weakest case: DIAL improves over base\_only (49.8\% vs.\
37.0\%) but falls far short of always\_trigger (59.8\%), a limitation analyzed in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

The main results show \emph{that} DIAL works; we now ask
\emph{why}. We ablate the two key components: direction learning
and the LLM feature layer.

\textbf{Direction is the primary determinant of gate quality.}
To isolate the effect of direction, we reverse the sign of all
learned weights in DIAL's fitted gate, keeping everything else
unchanged. Table~\ref{tab:wrong-direction} shows the result.
In environments with strong signal ($|\rho| > 0.4$), reversal
causes SR drops exceeding 23\,pp. In weak-signal environments
like Plancraft ($|\rho|{=}0.016$), reversal has negligible
effect. The degradation scales with signal strength: wrong
direction is not a minor calibration error but a structural
failure, consistent with the wrong-direction harm result of
Appendix~\ref{app:proof-necessity}. \emph{This table is the
direct empirical statement of the headline claim}: the gate
whose weights are reversed is, by construction, a more precisely
calibrated harm-targeting mechanism, and its 23--37\,pp SR
collapse on strong-signal environments is the price of that
precision when the direction is wrong.

\textbf{$\ell_1$ is not interchangeable with other shrinkage or
selection schemes.}
The signed-weight diagnostic of \S\ref{sec:learn} and DIAL's
tolerance of noisy LLM-proposed features both rely on the
\emph{exact} sparsity induced by $\ell_1$. Replacing it with
$\ell_2$ yields comparable SR on strong-signal environments but
eliminates the clean \{informative, uninformative\} partition,
because every feature retains a small non-zero weight; unregularized
logistic overfits on weak-signal environments like Plancraft; hard
mutual-information feature selection recovers SR on strong-signal
tasks but requires per-environment tuning of $k$, reintroducing the
engineering cost DIAL aims to eliminate. The full comparison across
regularizers, holding $\mathcal{D}$ and $\phi_{\text{cand}}$ fixed,
is reported in \S\ref{app:regularizer-ablation}.


\subsection{Two-Source Model Verification}
\label{sec:theory-verification}

The ablations confirm DIAL's practical advantage; we now test
whether the Two-Source Model (\S\ref{sec:toy-model}) has
predictive power beyond explaining the observed correlations
in Table~\ref{tab:signal-discovery}. Following
\citet{schaeffer2023emergent}, we derive three testable
predictions and verify each.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig_temporal_shift.pdf}
\caption{P1 verification: $\rho$(entropy, $U$) shifts from
early to late steps. Blue: early (step $\leq$ median), red:
late. Values on each bar show the Spearman $\rho$ for that
phase. Plancraft is omitted ($|\rho|{=}0.016$, too weak for
meaningful comparison).}
\label{fig:temporal-shift}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{P1: Temporal dynamics.}
The Two-Source Model predicts that $\rho$ should decrease over
the episode: as the agent gathers information, Type~D states
are resolved and late-step uncertainty increasingly reflects
residual Type~I states.
Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-shift} confirms this. In HotpotQA,
$\rho$ shifts from $-0.176$ (early) to $-0.437$ (late). In
WebShop, the strong positive early-phase signal
($\rho{=}{+}0.285$) vanishes in the late phase. In APPS,
$\rho$ reverses from positive ($+$0.102) to negative
($-$0.144). Full numerical results are in
Appendix~\ref{app:temporal}.

\paragraph{P2: Cross-setting consistency.}
The Two-Source Model predicts that settings with similar
$p_I$ should exhibit similar $\rho$. With the backbone held
fixed at Qwen3 (Table~\ref{tab:signal-discovery}), this holds:
FEVER and HotpotQA (both search-based QA) share negative $\rho$;
WebShop and APPS (both involving choice among alternatives)
share positive $\rho$. As predicted by the (environment,
backbone)-indexed mixture of \S\ref{sec:toy-model}, however,
task-structure similarity alone does not pin down $\rho$ when
the backbone changes: the same environment can sit on opposite
sides of $p_I^*$ under different LLMs
(Appendix~\ref{app:multi-backbone}).

\paragraph{P3: Signal identity alignment.}
The Two-Source Model predicts that the strongest signal should
reflect the dominant state type: information-sufficiency proxies
in Type~I environments, decision-complexity proxies in Type~D
environments. Appendix~\ref{app:signal-alignment} confirms
this: \texttt{step\_count} dominates in Type~I environments,
while \texttt{num\_available\_actions} dominates in WebShop.

To provide causal evidence, we manipulate information
availability within HotpotQA itself.
Table~\ref{tab:controlled} shows the results: restricting
evidence (InfoPoor) makes \texttt{step\_count} the dominant
signal ($\rho{=}{-}0.608$) while entropy is weak; injecting
gold evidence (InfoRich) makes entropy dominant
($\rho{=}{+}0.311$) while \texttt{step\_count} weakens.
This confirms that signal identity is a function of
information structure, not an environment-specific constant.
DIAL adapts to both conditions without manual reconfiguration.
