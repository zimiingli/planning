\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate EAAG along three axes: does environment-aware gating
Pareto-dominate fixed-direction baselines
(\S\ref{sec:main-results}); which components drive the gains
(\S\ref{sec:ablation}); and do the Two-Source Model's predictions
hold on held-out data (\S\ref{sec:theory-verification}).

\paragraph{Environments.} We evaluate on 8 environments spanning QA,
code generation, web navigation, fact verification, text games, and
crafting planning (Table~\ref{tab:env-setup}): HotpotQA,
APPS Intro, WebShop, FEVER, TWExpress, Plancraft, APPS Interview,
and CRUXEval. These environments are selected to cover the full
range of the Two-Source Model, from pure Type~I (FEVER) through
mixed ($\approx p_I^*$, APPS Intro) to Type~D (APPS Interview),
including extreme rollout properties (TWExpress: rollout-safe;
Plancraft: rollout-harmful).

\paragraph{Baselines.} All methods share the same agent and
optimizer $T$; only the gating decision differs. Bounds:
base\_only (never trigger), always\_trigger, oracle.
Fixed-direction baselines: CaTS, SEAG, CoRefine, CATTS, AUQ,
s1\_budget---all assuming high uncertainty warrants compute.
Ablation: BSW (direction intentionally reversed). Full
specifications in Appendix~\ref{app:experiments}.

\paragraph{Metrics.} SR (success rate, $\uparrow$) and Cost
(rollouts per episode during deployment, $\downarrow$).
A method Pareto-dominates another if $\text{SR} \geq$ and
$\text{Cost} \leq$ with at least one strict inequality.
Cost measures only the \emph{deployment} rollout budget; calibration
or exploration overhead is excluded for all methods, ensuring a fair
comparison of runtime efficiency.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}

\textbf{EAAG Pareto-dominates fixed-direction baselines.}
Table~\ref{tab:main} reports SR and cost across 8 environments
(summary in Table~\ref{tab:winloss}). EAAG matches or exceeds
every fixed-direction baseline in SR while using fewer rollouts;
the two exceptions occur in ceiling-effect environments where EAAG
already approaches the oracle. No calibration data is required
(statistical details in Appendix~\ref{app:additional}). Beyond
aggregate numbers, EAAG exhibits \emph{qualitatively different}
gating behavior: its trigger rate, direction, and signal weights
all adapt to the environment, as we analyze below.

\paragraph{Cross-environment patterns.}
Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} shows that the results align with the
Two-Source Model along three axes.
The gap between EAAG and baselines is largest where direction
mismatch is most severe: in Type~I environments, all ``high
uncertainty $\to$ trigger'' baselines fall below the no-trigger
baseline because the assumed direction is wrong---no amount of
calibration rescues them. Conversely, EAAG's advantage is smallest
in high-headroom environments where most methods approach the
oracle regardless of direction.
Figure~\ref{fig:entropy-bin} reveals the mechanism: in Type~I
environments, utility \emph{decreases} with entropy, while in
Type~D it increases---the same signal carries opposite meaning.
Most tellingly, EAAG adapts not
only direction but \emph{gating magnitude}: aggressive triggering
in rollout-safe environments (TWExpress, RR\,=\,73\%),
moderate triggering in mixed environments, and step-dependent
decay in rollout-harmful ones (Plancraft: 49\% at step~0 $\to$
$<$20\% later). This full behavioral range---from near-always
to near-never triggering---emerges from direction learning
alone, without explicit headroom estimation.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

The main results show \emph{that} EAAG works; we now ask
\emph{why}. We ablate the two components of EAAG that \S\ref{sec:signal-landscape}
identified as necessary: direction learning and the LLM feature
layer.

\textbf{Direction is the primary determinant of gate quality.}
Intentionally reversing the learned direction (BSW ablation) causes
catastrophic failure across environments, with an MLP gate using the
wrong direction falling \emph{below} the no-trigger baseline. This
confirms the central prediction of Proposition~\ref{prop:necessity}:
direction is not a minor calibration detail but the primary
determinant of gate quality. The magnitude of degradation correlates
with signal strength (Appendix~\ref{app:wrong-direction}), as the
Two-Source Model predicts.

\textbf{The LLM feature layer is an automation convenience, not
a core component.} Removing the LLM layer changes SR by
${<}$1\,pp in most environments; the gains concentrate in
settings like WebShop where task-specific signals meaningfully
extend the universal feature pool. In one environment LLM
features even introduce noise. Consistent with the framing in
\S\ref{sec:reason}, the LLM is best viewed as an automation
convenience that enables zero-shot deployment on new environments
rather than a component the method's main claims depend on.

\textbf{Gating magnitude emerges from direction learning alone.}
As Figure~\ref{fig:trigger-adapt} shows, the gate's trigger rate
varies from 73\% (TWExpress) to $<$20\% (late-step Plancraft),
with step-dependent decay in rollout-harmful environments. These
patterns are not explicitly programmed: they follow from the
per-environment signed weights fit in \S\ref{sec:learn}, which
capture each environment's signal--utility structure.
The cost of getting direction wrong is substantial:
Figure~\ref{fig:direction-cost} shows that BSW degradation
scales linearly with signal strength ($r{=}0.95$).

\subsection{Two-Source Model Verification}
\label{sec:theory-verification}

The ablations confirm EAAG's practical advantage; we now test
whether the Two-Source Model (\S\ref{sec:toy-model}) makes correct
predictions on held-out data. Following
\citet{schaeffer2023emergent}, we derive three testable predictions
and verify each:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=\textbf{P\arabic*:}]
\item \textbf{Temporal dynamics.} Within the same environment,
  $\rho$ should \emph{decrease} over the episode: early steps mix
  Type~I and Type~D uncertainty, while late steps, after
  information-gathering attempts, isolate the residual Type~I
  component.
\item \textbf{Cross-environment consistency.} Environments with
  similar task structure should exhibit similar $\rho$
  (e.g., FEVER $\approx$ HotpotQA, both search-based QA).
\item \textbf{Signal identity alignment.} In Type~I environments,
  the strongest signal should measure information sufficiency; in
  Type~D environments, decision complexity.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig_temporal_shift.pdf}
\caption{P1 verification: $\rho$(entropy, U) shifts from early to
late steps. Blue: early (step $\leq$ median), red: late.
$\Delta$ annotations show consistent shift toward the dominant
uncertainty type, confirming temporal dynamics prediction.}
\label{fig:temporal-shift}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{P1: Temporal dynamics (confirmed).}
Figure~\ref{fig:temporal-shift} shows the result. We split trajectory
data into early (step $\leq$ median) and late
(step $>$ median) subsets. In HotpotQA, $\rho$ shifts from $-0.176$
(early) to $-0.437$ (late): late-step entropy reflects deeper
information poverty after failed evidence retrieval. In APPS Intro,
$\rho$ shifts from $+0.102$ (early, non-significant) to $-0.144$
(late, $p{=}0.04$): early exploration diversity gives way to
debugging frustration. In WebShop, the strong positive early-phase
signal ($\rho{=}{+}0.285$) vanishes in the late phase. The pattern
is consistent across all environments with sufficient signal (full
data in Appendix~\ref{app:model-derivation}): early steps mix both
uncertainty sources, while late steps isolate the dominant type.

\paragraph{P2: Cross-environment consistency (confirmed).}
We compare same-family pairs. FEVER ($\rho{=}{-}0.119$) and HotpotQA
($\rho{=}{-}0.041$), both search-based QA tasks requiring evidence
retrieval, share negative entropy--utility direction.
APPS Intro ($\rho{=}{+}0.012$) and APPS Interview
($\rho{=}{+}0.317$), both code generation tasks, share non-negative
direction. The magnitude difference within each pair reflects the
degree of Type~I contamination; FEVER has more early-step
information poverty than HotpotQA.

\paragraph{P3: Signal identity alignment (confirmed).}
We examine the sparse-selected features per environment.
In Type~I environments (HotpotQA, FEVER), the strongest signal
(\texttt{step\_count}, $|\rho|{=}0.494$ and $0.619$) measures
trajectory progress, a proxy for information accumulation.
In WebShop (mixed, choice-dominated),
\texttt{num\_available\_actions} ($\rho{=}{+}0.444$) measures the
decision space size, a proxy for decision complexity. All three
predictions are confirmed, providing converging evidence for the
Two-Source Model.

As a supplementary test, we manipulate information availability
within HotpotQA itself (InfoPoor vs.\ InfoRich conditions) and
confirm that the identity of the most informative signal shifts
accordingly, consistent with the signal-identity finding in
\S\ref{sec:empirical-landscape}. Full results are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:controlled}.


\subsection{Robustness of Direction Reversal}
\label{sec:robustness}

Is direction reversal a genuine phenomenon or a confounding
artifact? We rule this out through three analyses, plus
multi-backbone verification (Appendix~\ref{app:multi-backbone})
showing that direction depends on the
\emph{environment~$\times$~model} interaction, not on either
factor alone.

\paragraph{Stratified analysis: controlling for trajectory length.}
Trajectory length varies across environments and correlates with both
entropy and utility. To rule out length as a confounder, we stratify
by step count and recompute $\rho(\text{entropy}, U)$ within each
stratum.
\emph{Result}: Direction reversal persists within trajectory-length
strata where utility variance is non-zero. In HotpotQA, $\rho$ remains
negative across all three strata ($-0.18$/$-0.46$/$-0.42$).
The reversal is not an artifact of trajectory length.

\paragraph{Interventional evidence.}
The BSW ablation (\S\ref{sec:ablation}) provides
\emph{interventional} evidence: reversing the gate's learned
direction causes catastrophic SR degradation across environments,
with the MLP gate falling below the no-trigger baseline. If
direction reversal were merely correlational, this manipulation
should have no systematic effect. The observed damage confirms
that direction encodes a causal relationship.

\paragraph{Gate complexity ablation: direction outweighs capacity.}
To verify that the bottleneck is direction rather than gate
complexity, we compare gate architectures with correct vs.\ wrong
direction (Table~\ref{tab:capacity}).

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Gate complexity ablation on HotpotQA. Direction matters
more than model capacity: a logistic gate with correct direction
outperforms an MLP with wrong direction by 49.9\,pp.}
\label{tab:capacity}
\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Gate & Direction & SR (\%) & $\Delta$ \\
\midrule
Logistic (5 feat) & Correct & 95.2 & +46.2 \\
MLP (5 feat)      & Correct & $\sim$95 & $\sim$+46 \\
Hidden state (2560-d) & Correct & $\sim$95 & $\sim$+46 \\
\midrule
Logistic (5 feat) & Wrong & 62.0 & +13.0 \\
MLP (5 feat)      & Wrong & 45.3 & $-$3.7 \\
\midrule
No gate (base)    & n/a & 49.0 & 0 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

With correct direction, all architectures achieve $\sim$95\% SR;
the jump from logistic to MLP to hidden-state probe adds $<$1\,pp.
With wrong direction, higher capacity makes performance \emph{worse}
(MLP 45.3\% $<$ logistic 62.0\%), because the more powerful gate
more precisely targets harmful states. Across the gate architectures
we tested, signal direction consistently outweighs both signal count
and gate complexity as a determinant of gate quality.
