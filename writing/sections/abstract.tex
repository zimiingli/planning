% Test-time compute improves decision quality for LLM agents, but deciding \emph{when} to invoke it across diverse environments remains an open problem. Existing adaptive-gating methods assume that higher uncertainty consistently indicates when extra computation helps. We show this assumption fails: the same uncertainty signal that predicts rollout benefit in one environment predicts rollout harm in another, and the sign can flip across model backbones with the task held fixed. When the assumed direction is wrong, sharper-ranking gates can \emph{worsen} performance by more precisely selecting harmful states. We trace this reversal to a mixture of \emph{information-poor} states, where rollouts cannot compensate for missing evidence, and \emph{decision-difficult} states, where rollouts productively explore alternatives. Under this two-source model, uncertainty alone is insufficient to guarantee non-negative value across heterogeneous settings. Motivated by this, we propose DIAL (\textbf{D}irection-\textbf{I}nformed \textbf{A}daptive \textbf{L}earning), a sparse gate trained from signal-agnostic counterfactual exploration to learn the utility direction of state features. Across six environments and three backbones, DIAL consistently outperforms fixed-direction baselines.
Test-time compute improves decision quality for LLM agents, but deciding \emph{when} to invoke it remains unreliable across diverse environments. Existing adaptive-gating methods assume that higher uncertainty consistently indicates when extra computation helps. We show this assumption fails: the signal--utility direction reverses across both environments and model backbones, with the task held fixed. When the assumed direction is wrong, more confident gates can \emph{worsen} performance by triggering more precisely on harmful states. We trace this reversal to a mixture of \emph{information-poor} states, where rollouts cannot compensate for missing evidence, and \emph{decision-difficult} states, where rollouts productively explore alternatives. Under this two-source model, uncertainty alone cannot reliably indicate when computation helps across heterogeneous settings. Motivated by this, we propose DIAL (\textbf{D}irection-\textbf{I}nformed \textbf{A}daptive \textbf{L}earning), a sparse gate trained from signal-agnostic counterfactual exploration to learn the utility direction of state features. Across six environments and three backbones, DIAL Pareto-dominates fixed-direction baselines in both success rate and cost. Our code is open-sourced at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DIAL-2A7E/}.
