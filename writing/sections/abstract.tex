% Test-time compute improves decision quality for LLM agents, but deciding \emph{when} to invoke it across diverse environments remains an open problem. Existing adaptive-gating methods assume that higher uncertainty consistently indicates when extra computation helps. We show this assumption fails: the same uncertainty signal that predicts rollout benefit in one environment predicts rollout harm in another, and the sign can flip across model backbones with the task held fixed. When the assumed direction is wrong, sharper-ranking gates can \emph{worsen} performance by more precisely selecting harmful states. We trace this reversal to a mixture of \emph{information-poor} states, where rollouts cannot compensate for missing evidence, and \emph{decision-difficult} states, where rollouts productively explore alternatives. Under this two-source model, uncertainty alone is insufficient to guarantee non-negative value across heterogeneous settings. Motivated by this, we propose DIAL (\textbf{D}irection-\textbf{I}nformed \textbf{A}daptive \textbf{L}earning), a sparse gate trained from signal-agnostic counterfactual exploration to learn the utility direction of state features. Across six environments and three backbones, DIAL consistently outperforms fixed-direction baselines.

Adaptive test-time compute for LLM agents aims to invoke extra computation only when it improves performance. Existing methods typically use confidence-, uncertainty-, or difficulty-based gates, assuming a fixed direction from the gating signal through compute need to the value of computation. This makes gating a utility-calibration problem: gating signals should align with whether extra computation improves the final outcome over the base policy. We show that this alignment is unstable: the same signal predicts rollout benefit in one setting and rollout harm in another, with reversals across environments and backbones even when the task is fixed. Wrong-direction gates can therefore worsen performance by precisely selecting harmful states.
This reversal reflects a deeper distinction between compute need and compute suitability: a high uncertainty signal may indicate decision-difficult states where rollouts help compare alternatives, or information-poor states where rollouts from the same context cannot repair missing evidence. Under this two-source model, fixed-direction gates are unreliable across heterogeneous settings. We propose DIAL (\textbf{D}irection-\textbf{I}nformed \textbf{A}daptive \textbf{L}earning), a sparse gate trained from signal-agnostic counterfactual exploration to learn the utility direction of state features per (environment, backbone). Across six environments and three backbones, DIAL yields a stronger overall success-cost trade-off than fixed-direction baselines. Our code is open-sourced at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DIAL-2A7E/}.
