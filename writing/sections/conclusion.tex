\section{Conclusion}

Existing adaptive gating methods for LLM agents assume that
uncertainty signals have a fixed relationship with compute value.
We show this assumption is a structural limitation: uncertainty
signals are not sufficient statistics for the value of
computation, because their meaning depends on a latent state
property (information poverty vs.\ decision difficulty) that the
signal alone cannot identify. Across six environments and three
model backbones, signal--utility direction reverses, and once
misaligned, better calibration \emph{worsens} performance by more
precisely selecting harmful states.
A two-source model grounded in Simpson's paradox explains when
and why reversal occurs, and a necessity proof establishes that
direction discovery is unavoidable for cross-environment
robustness. DIAL instantiates this principle with a minimal
pipeline that recovers per-environment direction through sparse
linear learning, adding zero per-step overhead at deployment.
Across all evaluated settings, DIAL dominates fixed-direction
baselines, and its full range of gating behavior emerges from
direction learning alone.
