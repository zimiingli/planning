\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Prior work on adaptive test-time compute spans diverse mechanisms
but rests on a shared assumption: that uncertainty signals have a
fixed, environment-independent relationship with compute value.

\paragraph{Gating on uncertainty signals.}
One line of work allocates extra computation based on token
confidence, entropy, or disagreement, under the assumption that
higher uncertainty indicates greater need for compute. In the
reasoning setting, signal-based methods extend chain-of-thought
generation when token confidence or entropy exceeds a
threshold~\cite{seag2025, cats2025, corefine2026, thinkjustenough2025,
s1_2025, han2025tokenbudget}. In the agent setting, vote-based
methods~\cite{catts2026} sample multiple candidate actions and
invoke an arbiter when they disagree, while verbalized uncertainty
methods~\cite{auq2026} prompt the agent to reflect when its
self-reported confidence is low.

\paragraph{Gating on learned difficulty.}
A second line learns to predict task difficulty and allocate compute
accordingly. Hidden-state probes~\cite{diffadapt2025,
zhu2025llmalreadyknows} estimate difficulty from internal
representations, and RL-based methods~\cite{adaptthink2025,
thinkless2025, aggarwal2025l1, paglieri2025learning} learn
think/no-think policies end-to-end. Though these methods do not
commit to a specific signal, their gating policies effectively
behave as if higher difficulty warrants more compute, sharing the
same fixed, environment-independent direction as the first family.
This assumption holds well in the settings where these methods have
been developed and evaluated, predominantly homogeneous reasoning
benchmarks such as MATH, GSM8K, and GPQA, where signal direction is
consistent by construction. Whether it transfers to heterogeneous
agent environments is an open question that prior work has not
directly addressed; we show it does not
(Proposition~\ref{prop:necessity}). A per-method comparison is in
Appendix~\ref{app:related}.

\paragraph{Orthogonal: search-based optimizers.}
Search methods such as Tree of Thoughts~\cite{yao2023tree},
LATS~\cite{zhou2024lats}, RAP~\cite{hao2023reasoning}, and
FLARE~\cite{wang2026flare} improve \emph{what} the optimizer does
through tree search, value propagation, and lookahead evaluation.
These are orthogonal to our work: we control \emph{when} to invoke
the optimizer, and our gate composes with any such procedure.

\paragraph{Metareasoning and uncertainty semantics.}
Rational metareasoning~\cite{russell1991right,
desabbata2024metareasoning} formalizes the value of computation
(VOC), classically assuming $\text{VOC} \geq 0$ since an agent can
disregard unfavorable computations. We show that this assumption
fails in the agent setting, where the same model both proposes and
evaluates actions: under this evaluator--executor identity, VOC can
be \emph{negative}, making direction discovery necessary.
\cite{tao2025revisiting} and \cite{heo2025llmuncertainty} find
that calibration quality varies across task types, and
\cite{yadkori2024believe} decouple epistemic and aleatoric
uncertainty---providing converging evidence that uncertainty
semantics are domain-dependent, though they stop short of
documenting direction reversal. Our two-source model formalizes
this domain dependence (\S\ref{sec:toy-model}), and our reversal
results make it actionable.
